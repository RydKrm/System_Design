## 9. Introduction — The Sense That Speaks Without Words {#intro-haptic}

Touch is the oldest of the human senses — evolutionarily, it precedes sight and hearing as a mechanism for understanding the world. Before the human infant can see clearly or distinguish speech from noise, it can feel — warmth, pressure, texture, the rhythm of a heartbeat. The sense of touch is processed by a dedicated neural pathway and registered in a part of the brain that is ancient, deep, and deeply connected to emotional response. When a device vibrates in a specific pattern in response to a specific action, it speaks directly to this ancient system, bypassing the conscious processing that visual signals often require and arriving directly at the level of felt confirmation.

This is why haptic feedback, when designed well, produces a satisfaction that is qualitatively different from visual feedback alone. It is not merely more information added to the same channel — it is information delivered through an entirely different and more intimate channel. The haptic confirmation of a successful payment does not merely tell the user that the payment succeeded in the way that a green checkmark does. It tells the body that something real just happened — that an event of significance has occurred, and the device is acknowledging it through the most direct sensory channel available. This is why Apple describes its Taptic Engine as producing "taps" rather than "vibrations" — the word "tap" implies a social gesture, a touch between two entities, in a way that "vibration" does not. A well-designed haptic pattern is a product reaching through the glass to touch the user back.

## 10. How Haptic Engines Work — The Physics of Touch {#how-haptic}

The haptic engines in modern smartphones are linear resonant actuators — small devices that use electromagnetic force to drive a weighted mass back and forth along a single axis at precisely controlled frequencies and amplitudes. Unlike the simple rotary vibration motors in older phones (which could only turn on and off, producing a buzzing vibration of fixed intensity), linear resonant actuators can be driven at specific frequencies for specific durations with specific intensity envelopes, producing a much wider range of tactile sensations with much greater precision and much faster response times.

Apple's Taptic Engine, first introduced in the iPhone 6s, achieves response times on the order of 10 milliseconds — fast enough to synchronise precisely with visual events without the user perceiving any gap between what they see and what they feel. This synchronisation is critical to the effectiveness of haptic feedback: a haptic that arrives 200 milliseconds after the visual confirmation it accompanies does not reinforce the visual; it creates a separate, confusing second event. Haptic and visual feedback must be synchronised within the threshold of multi-sensory integration — approximately 50 milliseconds — to be experienced as a single unified event rather than two separate ones.

The Taptic Engine's ability to produce distinct, characterised sensations — the crisp "click" of the .rigid haptic, the descending "thud-thud-thud" of the .error pattern, the ascending "success chime" of the .success pattern — arises from the precise control of three parameters: the frequency of oscillation (higher frequencies feel sharper and crisper; lower frequencies feel heavier and more diffuse), the amplitude of oscillation (higher amplitude feels stronger; lower amplitude feels subtler), and the duration and envelope of the oscillation (a sharp attack and rapid decay feels like a tap; a slow attack and gradual decay feels like a rumble).

## 11. The Haptic Vocabulary — Every Pattern and Its Meaning {#vocabulary}

Just as a visual design system establishes a consistent vocabulary of colours, shapes, and typographic styles that carry specific meanings, a haptic design system establishes a consistent vocabulary of tactile patterns that carry specific meanings. The effectiveness of this vocabulary depends entirely on its consistency: each pattern must map reliably to the same type of event or outcome across the entire product, so that users can internalise the mapping and eventually receive haptic information without needing to look at the screen to interpret it.

## 12. Haptic Design Principles — Proportionality and Intent {#haptic-principles}

The practice of haptic design is governed by principles that parallel the principles of visual design hierarchy. Just as not every visual element should have equal prominence, not every interaction should have equal haptic weight. The haptic intensity of a response must be proportional to the significance of the action it confirms, so that the haptic vocabulary communicates a hierarchy of importance to the hand in the same way that visual hierarchy communicates importance to the eye.

The first principle is **proportionality** — matching haptic intensity to action weight. A light tap on a list item warrants the lightest available haptic. A successful completion of a purchase warrants the richest positive haptic pattern. A fatal error — a payment failure, a data loss, an irreversible destructive action — warrants the most intense and most clearly negative haptic pattern. When proportionality is violated — when every interaction produces the same haptic intensity regardless of its significance — the haptic vocabulary loses its communicative value. The user's hand can no longer distinguish between "you just tapped a photo to view it" and "your payment of £500 was just processed." Both events feel the same. The haptic has become noise.

The second principle is **restraint** — using haptic feedback only when it adds genuine communicative value, not as decoration. The temptation to add haptic feedback to every animation, every scroll event, every visual transition is a temptation that should be firmly resisted. Haptic feedback that fires without a clear corresponding user action or system outcome is haptic feedback that the user will experience as meaningless vibration — and meaningless vibration very quickly becomes annoying vibration. Every haptic event should be directly traceable to a specific user action or system outcome, and its timing should be precisely synchronised with the visual feedback for that event.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         HAPTIC DESIGN PRINCIPLES — VISUAL SUMMARY               │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PRINCIPLE 1: PROPORTIONALITY
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  ACTION WEIGHT       HAPTIC INTENSITY    PATTERN
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Trivial             ●○○○○               .light (barely felt)
  (scroll snap, tick)

  Routine             ●●○○○               .medium (clear click)
  (button tap)

  Significant         ●●●○○               .heavy / .success
  (task complete)

  Critical/negative   ●●●●●               .error (unmistakable)
  (payment failed)

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PRINCIPLE 2: RESTRAINT — When NOT to use haptics
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  ✗ Scroll events (every scroll frame = vibration noise)
  ✗ Animations with no user action trigger
  ✗ Background events the user didn't initiate
  ✗ Decorative interactions (hover effects, colour changes)
  ✗ Rapid-fire actions < 200ms apart (haptic stacking = buzz)
  ✗ Every screen transition (transitions are visual events)

  ✓ User-initiated taps and presses
  ✓ State changes the user caused (toggle, select, submit)
  ✓ System responses to user actions (success, failure, error)
  ✓ Threshold moments in gestures (long-press, swipe complete)
  ✓ Physical simulation moments (drag pick-up, drop, snap)

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PRINCIPLE 3: SYNCHRONISATION
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Haptic and visual must fire within 50ms of each other.
  Outside 50ms → user perceives them as separate events.
  Separate events = confusing. Unity = convincing.

  WRONG:                       RIGHT:
  Tap → [0ms] → visual change  Tap → [0ms] → visual change
       → [200ms] → haptic            → [0ms] → haptic (simultaneous)
  User feels: two events.      User feels: one unified event.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PRINCIPLE 4: USER RESPECT — Haptics must be disableable
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Always honour system haptic settings.
  If user disables haptics in system settings → your app
  should produce zero haptic output. Non-negotiable.
  Never override system preferences.
  Offer in-app haptic intensity control for haptic-rich apps.
```

## 14. Haptic Failures and Fixes {#haptic-failures}

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         HAPTIC FAILURES — DIAGNOSIS & FIX                       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FAILURE 1: NO HAPTICS ON MOBILE (most common)
  Symptom: App produces zero haptic feedback. Feels flat, digital,
           less premium than native apps.
  Cause:   Haptics never implemented. Tested only on simulator.
  Fix:     Implement at minimum: .medium for primary buttons,
           .success for successful completions, .error for failures.
           Test on physical device before each release.

  FAILURE 2: SAME HAPTIC FOR EVERY ACTION
  Symptom: Every tap, every toggle, every error = identical buzz.
           Haptic vocabulary has no semantic meaning.
  Cause:   Single haptic call used universally.
  Fix:     Map haptic to action type. Light for trivial.
           Heavy for significant. Error for failures. Consistent.

  FAILURE 3: HAPTIC FIRES ON EVERY SCROLL FRAME
  Symptom: Scrolling through a list → constant vibration.
           Irritating within seconds. User disables haptics.
  Cause:   Haptic attached to scroll event instead of snap point.
  Fix:     Haptic on SNAP TO ITEM (discrete), not on continuous
           scroll movement. .selection on item snap only.

  FAILURE 4: HAPTIC NOT SYNCHRONISED WITH VISUAL
  Symptom: User taps button. Visual changes at 0ms.
           Haptic fires at 150ms. Feels like two events.
  Cause:   Haptic call in callback after async operation.
  Fix:     Fire haptic synchronously on user gesture event,
           not in the callback. Haptic = response to gesture,
           not to operation completion (use notification haptic for that).

  FAILURE 5: HAPTIC OVERRIDES SYSTEM SETTINGS
  Symptom: User has disabled haptics in iOS/Android settings.
           App still vibrates. User is frustrated.
  Cause:   Direct vibration call bypassing system preferences.
  Fix:     Always use platform haptic APIs (UIFeedbackGenerator,
           HapticFeedbackConstants). These automatically respect
           system haptic settings. Never use raw vibration for UI.

  FAILURE 6: HAPTIC STACKING (rapid repeated taps)
  Symptom: User taps button rapidly 5 times. Device buzzes
           continuously. Haptic becomes noise, not signal.
  Cause:   No cooldown between haptic events.
  Fix:     Disable button after first tap (prevent repetition).
           If tapping IS valid: minimum 200ms between haptic events.

  FAILURE 7: WRONG HAPTIC PATTERN FOR OUTCOME VALENCE
  Symptom: Success confirmation fires .error haptic.
           Or: Error state fires .success haptic.
           Haptic contradicts visual. Deeply confusing.
  Cause:   Copy-paste error. Wrong constant used.
  Fix:     Audit all haptic calls. Positive outcomes (.success),
           negative outcomes (.error). No exceptions.
```
