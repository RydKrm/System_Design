## 15. Introduction — The Language of Movement {#intro-timing}

An interface without transitions is an interface that teleports. One moment a screen exists; the next moment a different screen exists; and between these two states there is nothing — no movement, no connection, no story of how the world got from one state to the other. The user's mind, which instinctively constructs spatial models of the environments it navigates, is given no material from which to construct a spatial relationship between the two screens. They are simply separate places, arrived at by teleportation rather than travel.

Transitions solve this problem by providing the connective tissue between states — the visual narrative of movement from one place to another. A card that expands from the list to fill the screen tells the user where the detail view came from and how to get back. A sheet that slides up from the bottom of the screen tells the user that this is a supplementary context, overlaid on what was there before, and that the original context is still below. A navigation push that moves the new screen in from the right and the old screen out to the left tells the user they have moved forward in a hierarchy. Each of these transitions is a spatial communication — a micro-story that locates the user in a structure they can understand and navigate.

But the quality of a transition's communication depends as much on its timing as on its direction or its type. A transition that is too fast — that completes in 80 milliseconds — is a transition that provides no time for the spatial story to be registered. It is faster than the eye can follow, and a transition too fast to follow tells the brain nothing about spatial relationships. A transition that is too slow — that takes 800 milliseconds for a routine screen change — is a transition that interrupts the user's flow, making them wait for the animation to complete before they can proceed. It is as if every door in a building had a hydraulic closer set to close with exceptional slowness — the door works, but every passage through it is an imposition.

## 16. The Science of Time Perception in Interfaces {#time-perception}

The scientific understanding of how humans perceive time in the context of interface interactions is built from decades of research in both cognitive psychology and human-computer interaction. The most foundational of these findings — Robert Miller's 1968 paper establishing the 0.1 second, 1 second, and 10 second thresholds for different categories of system response — has been extensively refined and supplemented by subsequent research, but its core insights remain the foundation of all timing design.

Miller's research established that the human perception of computer response time falls into three distinct zones, each of which produces a different subjective experience and a different set of user expectations. The first zone, below 100 milliseconds, is the zone of perceived immediacy — responses within this zone feel like direct physical consequences of the user's action, indistinguishable in quality from the response of a physical device. The second zone, between 100 milliseconds and approximately 1 second, is the zone of perceived responsiveness — responses in this range feel fast but distinctly digital, clearly separate from the user's action but close enough in time to maintain the flow of thought. The third zone, above 1 second, is the zone of perceived delay — responses taking longer than 1 second interrupt the user's thought flow and require them to consciously wait for the system.

For transition animations specifically, research by the Google Material Design team and by independent researchers at institutions including Stanford's HCI group has established a more granular set of recommendations. Transitions for small elements — a single icon changing state, a single field gaining focus — should be the fastest, in the range of 100–200 milliseconds. Transitions for medium elements — a card expanding, a modal sliding in — should be in the range of 250–350 milliseconds. Transitions for large elements — full-page transitions, complex layout changes — should be in the range of 300–500 milliseconds. And all transitions should use easing curves that reflect the physical behaviour of objects in motion — ease-out for responses (arriving), ease-in for departures (leaving), and spring physics for interactions that benefit from a sense of physical elasticity.

## 17. Easing — The Curves That Give Motion Its Character {#easing}

Easing is the characteristic of an animation that describes how the rate of change varies over the animation's duration. A linear animation changes at a constant rate from start to finish — moving the same distance in each equal unit of time. An eased animation accelerates or decelerates over its duration, changing its rate in a way that mimics the behaviour of physical objects. Easing is the single most important determinant of whether an animation feels natural and physical or mechanical and digital, and it is the most frequently misunderstood aspect of motion design.

The reason easing matters so deeply is rooted in how human beings have learned to interpret movement. We have spent our entire lives observing objects in the physical world — objects that accelerate when pushed, decelerate under friction, and respond to gravity. The laws of physics govern how every physical object moves, and our visual system has been calibrated over a lifetime of observation to expect movement that follows these laws. When digital animations respect the same principles of acceleration and deceleration, they feel physically real — they feel like things moving in space, not like pixels being repositioned. When digital animations use linear timing — moving at a constant rate — they feel alien. They do not correspond to anything in the physical world. Everything in the physical world that moves, decelerates.

The most commonly used easing curves in interface design are derived from the cubic Bézier function — a mathematical formula that defines a curve between two control points. Four of these curves have become standard in interface design: ease-out (fast start, slow end — for responses and arrivals), ease-in (slow start, fast end — for departures and dismissals), ease-in-out (slow start, fast end, slow end — for crossfades and state changes where both the departure and arrival should feel smooth), and the spring function (an oscillating curve that overshoots the target and settles — for interactions that should feel physical and elastic).

## 18. Duration — How Long Is Right? {#duration}

Duration is the dimension of transition timing that designers most frequently get wrong, and it is wrong in both directions — transitions too fast to register and transitions too slow to tolerate are equally common problems. The correct duration for a transition is a function of three variables: the physical size of the element being animated (larger elements should take longer to traverse the screen), the complexity of the animation (more complex multi-element animations need more time for each part to be understood), and the importance of the transition's communicative content (transitions that carry important spatial information about screen relationships should be slow enough to register).

The most critical general rule of transition duration is that **the animation must never make the user wait**. A transition that the user must watch complete before they can proceed is a transition that has become friction. The transition's purpose is to communicate — to inform the spatial model without interrupting the flow. If the transition takes longer to complete than the user needs to absorb its spatial information, the excess duration is pure friction. And the duration of time required to absorb spatial information is, research suggests, approximately 200–250 milliseconds for most transitions — a figure that explains why the most commonly recommended transition duration range for standard UI transitions is 250–350 milliseconds.

## 20. The Relationship Between Timing, Hierarchy, and Meaning {#hierarchy-timing}

Transition timing is not merely a technical parameter — it is a communicative tool that establishes hierarchy and conveys meaning. The duration and character of a transition communicates to the user how significant the change being represented is, how deep in the product's spatial hierarchy it is occurring, and how they should orient themselves in relation to it.

Slow transitions communicate importance. A transition that takes 400 milliseconds is, by its duration alone, telling the user that something meaningful is happening — that the change being represented is significant enough to warrant four tenths of a second of deliberate attention. Fast transitions communicate routine. A 150-millisecond crossfade says "this is just a small state change — carry on." This is the principle of **temporal hierarchy** — using duration as a signal of significance in the same way that size, colour, and position communicate visual hierarchy.

The direction and character of a transition communicates spatial relationships. A slide from right communicates forward movement in a hierarchy. A slide from bottom communicates a temporary overlay. A crossfade communicates a contextual change within the same level. Spring physics communicate physical interaction — the object has mass and responds to forces. Each of these communicative conventions has been established through the accumulated experience of digital product design across two decades of touchscreen interaction, and users have internalised them to a degree that violating them feels immediately and deeply wrong, even if the violation cannot be specifically articulated.

## 21. Timing Failures and Fixes {#timing-failures}

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         TRANSITION TIMING FAILURES — DIAGNOSIS GUIDE            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FAILURE 1: ALL TRANSITIONS AT LINEAR EASING
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Interface moves but feels robotic, digital, unnatural.
           Users describe it as "stiff" or "old-fashioned."
  Cause:   Default CSS transition with no cubic-bezier specified.
           All transitions use linear (browser default in some cases).
  Fix:     Apply ease-out to all arriving elements.
           Apply ease-in to all departing elements.
           Never use linear for directional transitions.

  FAILURE 2: TOO-FAST TRANSITIONS (< 100ms for complex transitions)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Screens seem to snap between states. No spatial story.
           Users feel disoriented. "Where did that come from?"
  Cause:   All transitions set to very short duration for "speed."
  Fix:     150ms minimum for micro transitions.
           250ms minimum for navigation transitions.
           Faster is not always better — spatial story needs time.

  FAILURE 3: TOO-SLOW TRANSITIONS (> 500ms for routine changes)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Users wait for animations to complete. Interaction
           feels slow despite fast underlying performance.
           Users describe app as "sluggish" or "heavy."
  Cause:   Animations prioritised for beauty over speed.
  Fix:     Cap routine transitions at 300–350ms.
           Navigation max 400ms. Anything longer = special case.
           Test: Can user interact before animation completes? They will try.

  FAILURE 4: SAME DURATION FOR ALL TRANSITION TYPES
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Small state changes take same time as full page nav.
           No temporal hierarchy. Everything feels equally significant.
  Cause:   Single transition duration variable used everywhere.
  Fix:     Design a timing scale with at least 3 tiers:
           Micro (100–200ms): component state changes
           Standard (250–350ms): navigation, modals
           Deliberate (400–500ms): onboarding, brand moments

  FAILURE 5: ENTER AND EXIT SAME DURATION
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Exiting elements feel slow. Users wait for them to leave.
  Cause:   Enter and exit durations set identically.
  Fix:     Exit = 75% of enter duration.
           300ms enter → 225ms exit. Every time.

  FAILURE 6: DIRECTION MISMATCH
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Navigating "forward" in an app produces a leftward slide.
           Going "back" produces a rightward slide entering from left.
           User spatial model breaks. They feel disoriented.
  Cause:   Transition direction not mapped to spatial hierarchy.
  Fix:     Forward nav → new screen enters from RIGHT.
           Back nav → new screen enters from LEFT.
           Supplementary → enters from BOTTOM.
           Global overlay → fade (no direction).

  FAILURE 7: NO prefers-reduced-motion SUPPORT
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Symptom: Users with vestibular disorders experience nausea,
           dizziness, or motion sickness from animated transitions.
           Accessibility complaint. Potential legal issue.
  Cause:   Animations not conditionally disabled for users who
           have enabled "Reduce Motion" in system settings.
  Fix:     @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
             * { transition-duration: 0.01ms !important;
                 animation-duration: 0.01ms !important; }
           }
           For iOS: UIAccessibility.isReduceMotionEnabled
           For Android: ANIMATOR_DURATION_SCALE system setting
```

            Is the duration appropriate for the element's size?
            Does it communicate spatial relationships correctly?
  
  ALL THREE working together = an interaction that feels real.
  Any one missing = an interaction that feels incomplete.
  All three wrong = an interaction that feels broken.

## Conclusion — The Three Channels of Truth

Loading animations, haptic feedback, and transition timing are three separate disciplines united by a single purpose: making the invisible visible, the intangible tangible, and the instantaneous comprehensible. Together they form the three sensory channels through which a digital product tells its users the truth — the truth that their action was received, that work is being done, that the result has arrived, that the direction of movement means what they think it means.

Loading animations speak through the eye to the part of the mind that tracks time. They say: "You are not abandoned. Something is happening. Here is what it looks like to wait for something good." Haptic feedback speaks through the hand to the part of the nervous system that processes physical reality. It says: "This was real. It happened in the world. Your body knows it." Transition timing speaks through motion to the part of the mind that builds spatial models. It says: "You moved from there to here. This is what that movement looked like. You understand where you are."

Each channel is, on its own, a significant improvement over an interface that uses it not at all. But the magic of the three together — the loading state that communicates through both structure and shimmer, confirmed by a haptic at the moment of trigger, resolved through a transition that moves with the physics of something real — is an experience qualitatively different from the sum of its parts. It is the experience of a product that feels alive. Not alive in the science-fiction sense, but alive in the way that a well-made mechanical watch feels alive — because every part of it is doing its job with precision and intention, and the result is a harmony that transcends function and enters the territory of craft.

That territory is where the best digital products live. It is where users stop noticing the interface and start experiencing only what the interface exists to enable. And the path to it, it turns out, runs directly through the milliseconds — through the careful calibration of how long things take, what they feel like when they happen, and what they look like while the user waits.
