## Preface — The Map You Were Never Shown

When a designer ships a page, they have a mental image of how users will experience it. The headline will be read first. The hero image will communicate the product's value. The features section will be scanned methodically. The call-to-action will be found effortlessly. The testimonials will resolve any lingering doubts. This mental image is not fabricated from nothing — it is constructed from design expertise, visual hierarchy principles, competitive research, and genuine care about the user experience. It is usually coherent. And it is, with remarkable regularity, wrong in its specifics.

Real users do not read pages the way designers imagine them being read. They skip sections that designers considered essential. They click on things that designers never intended to be interactive. They abandon flows at precisely the moments that felt frictionless in the designer's mental simulation. They ignore the testimonials. They try to interact with a decorative element on the hero image. They make it five seconds down a thirty-second page and decide the product is not for them based on something in those first five seconds that the designer did not prioritize.

Click maps, scroll maps, and move maps are the instruments that replace the designer's mental image with actual evidence. They do not eliminate the need for design expertise or qualitative understanding — as the final chapter of this guide makes clear, they have real limits. But they provide something that expertise and imagination cannot: the unedited behavioral reality of hundreds or thousands of real users interacting with the actual product, captured in a visual form that is directly legible to a designer. They are the honest answer to the question every designer should be asking after every launch: "What actually happened when real people used this page?"

## 1. Why Three Maps and Not One

### Three Behaviors, Three Instruments

The reason that click maps, scroll maps, and move maps exist as three distinct instruments — rather than one unified behavioral map — is that they capture three fundamentally different dimensions of user behavior, each of which tells a different story about the page's effectiveness and each of which requires different interpretation skills.

**Clicking** is the most deliberate and consequential behavior captured by these tools. A click represents a decision — the user chose to engage with a specific element at a specific moment. It is the most direct signal of intent available in behavioral data. A user who clicks a CTA button has made a decision. A user who clicks a non-interactive element has made a decision too — the wrong one, from the interface's perspective, but a decision with significant design implications. Click data tells the story of where decisions happen on a page.

**Scrolling** is the behavior of navigation and discovery — the motion through which users move through content, deciding moment by moment whether to continue or abandon. It is less deliberate than clicking but no less important. The scroll map's primary revelation is the distribution of attention across the vertical extent of a page — how far into the content most users travel before their engagement ends. This is the map that most directly answers the question: "Do users see the content we designed?"

**Mouse movement** (on desktop) is the least deliberate behavior of the three — users move their cursor constantly and largely without conscious intention. But this unconscious motion is precisely what makes it valuable: because it is not deliberate, it is less subject to performance or social desirability bias. The move map is the closest proxy for eye gaze that can be captured without expensive eye-tracking hardware, and it reveals the reading patterns, visual scanning paths, and areas of hovering attention that neither clicks nor scrolls can show.

## 2. Click Maps — Where Decisions Happen

### The Technical Generation of a Click Map

A click map is generated by a JavaScript tracking library that records the screen coordinates of every click or tap event that occurs within a browser session on a defined page or set of pages. These coordinate records are aggregated across all sessions captured during a defined time window and rendered as a semi-transparent color overlay on a static screenshot of the page. The color at each coordinate represents the density of clicks at that location — with red indicating the highest concentrations and blue indicating the lowest.

The implementation is typically a few lines of JavaScript snippet embedded in the page — the same snippet that powers session recordings and other behavioral tracking. The data collection happens entirely in the background, without user awareness or participation, which is precisely what makes it reliable: users are not performing for an observer and their clicks represent authentic choices rather than responses to being watched.

### What a Click Map Actually Records

It is important to understand that a click map does not record only clicks on interactive elements. It records every click or tap that occurs anywhere on the page, regardless of whether that location contains an interactive element or not. This is not a limitation — it is one of the most valuable properties of click maps, because clicks on non-interactive regions of a page are some of the most diagnostically significant data points in the entire behavioral dataset.

When a user clicks on a static piece of text, a decorative image, or an empty region of the page, they are revealing an expectation — a belief that clicking in that region would do something. The design failed to communicate that the region was non-interactive. Or, more likely, the design created a visual signal that implied interactivity where none existed: an underlined word that looks like a link but is not, a button-shaped decorative element that is a visual rather than a functional element, a product screenshot that the user believed was a live demo they could interact with.

## 3. Reading a Click Map — The Full Interpretation Language

### Eight Patterns and What Each Means

Click map data speaks in patterns. A single click at an unusual location is noise. A cluster of clicks at an unusual location — a pattern that persists across multiple users and multiple sessions — is signal. The interpretation of a click map is fundamentally the practice of identifying these patterns, understanding why they exist, and determining whether each pattern represents desired behavior (and should be reinforced) or problematic behavior (and should be investigated and resolved).

```

  │  🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵🔵    │

  Meaning:  Users are reading and clicking on anxiety-resolving

            users want this information — give it to them more
            visibly so they convert instead of navigating away.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  PATTERN 7: FEATURE AREA WITH ZERO CLICKS ❌
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Description: A section designed to highlight features or

  │  [Feature 1 Card] 🔵  [Feature 2 Card] 🔵  [Feature 3] 🔵  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Meaning:  Users are either not reaching this section
            (check scroll map — do they even see it?), or they
            are reaching it but not engaging with it at all.
            Neither outcome means this section is working.
  Action:   Cross-reference with scroll map. If users reach the
            section but don't click: the section lacks a clear
            interactive call, or the content is not compelling

  PATTERN 8: DISTRIBUTED HEAT ACROSS TESTIMONIALS ✅

            names, roles, specific outcomes).

## 4. Rage Clicks — The Map's Most Urgent Signal

### When Frustration Becomes Visible

Rage clicks — the pattern of four, five, six, or more rapid clicks on the same location within a few seconds — are the most emotionally significant data point in a click map. They are not merely a navigation error or an architectural oversight. They are evidence of active, escalating user frustration: a user who expected something to happen, saw nothing happen, clicked again, and again, and again, with increasing urgency. The visual pattern in a click map looks like a dense, tight cluster of dots concentrated in a very small area — a heat signature that is qualitatively different from the broader, more diffuse clusters of normal intentional clicking.

Most heatmap tools now automatically detect and separately flag rage clicks, allowing designers to view only the rage click data rather than having it blended into the normal click map. This separate view is enormously useful because it prioritizes the investigation: a rage click cluster on a specific element is an almost certain indicator of a broken or misleading interaction that is generating user frustration, and it should be investigated and resolved before any other optimization work on that page.

## 5. Scroll Maps — The Geography of Attention Depth

### How Scroll Maps Are Generated

A scroll map is generated differently from a click map. Rather than recording discrete coordinate events (click at x, y), a scroll map records the **maximum scroll depth** reached by each user during their session on a given page — the furthest point in the vertical extent of the page that the user's viewport reached before they either converted (clicked something, submitted a form) or left the page. This maximum scroll depth is recorded as a percentage of the page's total height: 0% means the user never scrolled at all, 50% means they scrolled halfway down, 100% means they reached the bottom.

These scroll depth records are aggregated across all users who visited the page, and the aggregate is rendered as a color gradient overlay on the page: sections that were seen by 100% of users are fully rendered; sections that were seen by 50% are rendered at half opacity; sections seen by only 10% of users are nearly transparent. The visual result is a page that fades from fully visible at the top to nearly invisible at the bottom — a literal representation of how much of the page's content is actually reaching user eyes.

### The Fold — Myth and Reality

The concept of "above the fold" — the portion of a page visible without scrolling, derived from the newspaper convention of the most important content appearing above the physical fold of the newspaper — is one of the most debated concepts in web design. Some designers argue it is obsolete in an era of universally scroll-literate users; others argue it remains the most critical real estate on any page. The scroll map provides the empirical evidence to adjudicate this debate, and the evidence consistently shows that the fold is not a hard wall but a strong gradient — attention drops significantly after the fold but does not disappear entirely.

The specific numbers vary by page type, user intent, and design quality, but a typical well-designed landing page sees roughly 15–20% of users leave without scrolling past the initial viewport. This means that 80–85% of users do scroll — which is higher than the "nobody scrolls" mythology suggests. But it also means that the content visible without scrolling is the only content seen by 100% of users, and that every additional scroll increment is seen by progressively fewer.

## 7. Move Maps — The Invisible Eye

### What Mouse Movement Reveals

Move maps (sometimes called mouse-tracking maps or hover maps) capture the continuous position of the user's mouse cursor as it moves across the page during a session, independent of whether any clicks occur. The aggregate of all cursor positions across all sessions, rendered as a heat overlay, shows the areas of the page where the cursor spent the most accumulated time — and because the cursor tends to follow the eye's focus during active reading and consideration, this is approximately where users were looking.

The move map is the only instrument of the three that can reveal **reading behavior** — the way users scan text, which words their eye lingers on, which sections they re-read, and which sections their cursor passes through without pausing. This makes it particularly valuable for diagnosing the performance of text-heavy content: feature descriptions, testimonials, FAQ sections, and pricing explanations. The click map cannot distinguish between text that was read and text that was skipped — both look the same (no clicks). The scroll map shows the user reached the section but not what they did within it. The move map fills this gap.

## 9. Device-Specific Maps — Desktop vs. Mobile vs. Tablet

### Why You Must Never Aggregate Across Devices

One of the most consequential mistakes in heatmap analysis is viewing aggregate data across all device types simultaneously. Desktop and mobile users have fundamentally different physical interactions with the same page — they use different input devices, they have different viewport dimensions, the page often renders differently, and they are in different contexts of use. Aggregating their behavioral data produces a blended view that accurately represents neither population.

On desktop, users navigate with a mouse — a precise pointing device that generates continuous cursor position data (the basis of move maps) and allows precise clicking. On mobile, users navigate with their fingers — larger, less precise contact points that generate tap data (not continuous position data, so mobile move maps do not exist) and that interact with pages rendered at a different width and with different touch-optimized elements. On tablet, the situation is intermediate but distinct from both.

```

## 10. Segment-Specific Maps — The Same Page, Different Audiences

### How Different User Types Read the Same Page

Just as aggregating across devices produces misleading data, aggregating across user segments can obscure critical differences in how different populations experience the same page. A landing page visited by users from paid search ads, users from organic search, returning users who know the product, and users referred by a partner may produce four dramatically different behavioral maps when viewed separately. The aggregate of all four may look coherent and acceptable while masking serious problems within specific segments.

## 11. The Three Maps Together — A Combined Reading Method

### How to Read All Three as a Single System

The click map, scroll map, and move map answer different questions, but they are most powerful when read together — each answering the questions the others leave open, each providing context that makes the others' signals interpretable. A designer who reads all three simultaneously on the same page is conducting a form of behavioral triangulation: if all three maps agree that a specific area is either performing well or failing, the conclusion is robust. If they seem to contradict each other, the apparent contradiction itself is a diagnostic signal worth investigating.

## 12. From Maps to Hypotheses — The Action Framework

### Turning Visual Data Into Design Decisions

Reading the maps correctly is only half the work. The other half is converting the observations into specific, testable design hypotheses — precise claims about what is failing and why, with specific proposed changes and predicted outcomes. Without this translation, the map analysis is an academic exercise. With it, the map becomes the first step in a systematic improvement cycle.

```
THE HYPOTHESIS GENERATION FRAMEWORK
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HYPOTHESIS TEMPLATE:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "We observed [SPECIFIC MAP SIGNAL] on [PAGE/SECTION].
   We believe this is because [ROOT CAUSE DIAGNOSIS].
   We hypothesize that [SPECIFIC DESIGN CHANGE] will result in
   [EXPECTED BEHAVIORAL OUTCOME] because [REASONING].
   We will measure success by [SPECIFIC METRIC]."

  EXAMPLE HYPOTHESES FROM MAP OBSERVATIONS:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  OBSERVATION 1 (Click Map):
  The "Pricing" nav link receives 18% of all clicks on the landing
  page, more than the primary CTA (12% of clicks).

  HYPOTHESIS:
  "We observed that the Pricing navigation link receives more
   clicks than the CTA on our landing page. We believe this is
   because users are looking for pricing information that is not
   visible without scrolling to Section 7, making the nav link
   their fastest path. We hypothesize that adding a pricing
   summary module above the fold (showing the starting price
   and free tier) will reduce Pricing nav clicks by >50% while
   increasing primary CTA clicks by >20%, because users will
   no longer need to navigate away to find price information.
   We will measure success by CTA click rate and Pricing nav
   click rate after the change."

  OBSERVATION 2 (Scroll Map):
  Only 22% of users reach the pricing section at 88% scroll depth.

  HYPOTHESIS:
  "We observed that only 22% of landing page visitors reach the
   pricing section. We believe this is because the pricing section
   is too far below the fold for most visitors who make their
   decision earlier. We hypothesize that adding a pricing summary
   card at 40% scroll depth (within the features section) will
   increase the % of users who see pricing information from 22%
   to >60%, and will either increase conversion (if pricing is
   competitive) or reduce time-wasted traffic (if pricing
   filters out non-ICP visitors). We will measure success by
   % of sessions that scroll past the pricing summary and by
   trial signup conversion rate."

  OBSERVATION 3 (Move Map):
  The feature description section shows rapid transit cursor
  movement — near-zero hover time despite being reached by
  58% of users.

  HYPOTHESIS:
  "We observed that the features section is reached by 58% of
   users but the move map shows near-zero reading behavior.
   Users are scrolling through it without reading. We believe
   this is because the feature titles are functional labels
   ('Advanced Analytics', 'Workflow Automation') rather than
   outcome statements, and do not arrest the scanning eye.
   We hypothesize that rewriting feature titles as outcome
   statements ('See exactly where projects stall in real time',
   'Automate handoffs between teams without Zapier') will
   increase cursor dwelling time in the features section by
   >40% and increase CTA clicks that originate after the
   features section by >15%. We will measure success using
   move map heat in the features section and click attribution."

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## 14. The Limits of the Maps — What They Cannot Show

### Reading With Appropriate Humility

Click maps, scroll maps, and move maps are extraordinarily useful. They are also limited in specific, important ways. A designer who reads them without understanding their limits will over-interpret them — drawing confident conclusions from data that is ambiguous, correlational, or incomplete. The final chapter of this guide is a deliberate accounting of those limits.

```
THE LIMITS OF BEHAVIORAL MAPS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LIMIT 1: MAPS SHOW WHAT, NOT WHY
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A red heat cluster tells you where attention or clicks
  concentrated. It does not tell you why users clicked there,
  what they expected to happen, how they felt when they did
  or didn't get a response, or what would change if you
  redesigned the element.

  The "why" requires qualitative research:
  Session recordings → observe the specific behavior
  Exit surveys       → ask the user directly
  User interviews    → probe the motivation in depth

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  LIMIT 2: MAPS ARE STATISTICAL COMPOSITES — THEY HIDE DIVERSITY
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A click map is the average behavior of all users who visited
  the page in a time window. It hides the fact that organic
  search users and paid social users may have behaved very
  differently. New visitors and returning visitors may have
  contradictory scroll patterns that, averaged together,
  look like a third pattern that accurately represents neither.

  Mitigation: Always segment before drawing conclusions.
  (See Chapter 10)

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  LIMIT 3: SCROLL MAPS RECORD MAX DEPTH, NOT READING TIME
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A scroll map records that a user scrolled to 80% depth.
  It does not record:
  → Whether they read the content at 80% depth
  → How long they spent at each depth
  → Whether they scrolled back UP after reaching 80%
  → Whether they opened a tab and the scroll position was
    automated by a browser restore

  A user who auto-scrolled through a page is recorded
  identically to one who read every line.

  Mitigation: Cross-reference with move map (which does show
  reading time) and with session recordings.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  LIMIT 4: MOVE MAPS ARE DESKTOP-ONLY AND IMPRECISE
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  On mobile: There is no cursor. Move maps do not exist.
  The majority of web traffic is now mobile. For many
  products, the move map represents the minority of sessions.

  Even on desktop: The cursor-eye correlation (r = 0.72–0.84)
  is useful but not precise. Users frequently look at areas
  their cursor is not near. Some users keep their cursor
  stationary while reading. Some move their cursor constantly
  regardless of where they are looking.

  Mitigation: Treat move maps as directional evidence, not
  precise eye-tracking. Confirm important move map conclusions
  with actual eye-tracking studies if the stakes are high.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  LIMIT 5: MAPS CAPTURE SURFACE BEHAVIOR, NOT DECISION QUALITY
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A high CTA click rate on a click map looks like success.
  It may be genuine — users who understand the product and
  want to try it. Or it may be a manipulative dark pattern —
  a button designed to be clicked before users understand
  what they are agreeing to. The click map cannot tell the
  difference. It records the click.

  The quality of a conversion — whether the user who clicked
  understood what they were getting into, whether they remain
  satisfied after their click, whether they become a loyal
  customer or an immediate churn — is invisible to the map.

  Mitigation: Always track downstream quality metrics
  alongside map-derived conversion improvements. A higher
  click-through rate that produces lower-quality activations
  is not a design success.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  THE FUNDAMENTAL TRUTH ABOUT ALL THREE MAPS:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                                 │
  │  Click maps, scroll maps, and move maps are the fastest         │
  │  and most scalable windows into user behavior available.        │
  │  They should be the starting point of every design              │
  │  investigation — not the ending point.                          │
  │                                                                 │
  │  They tell you WHERE to look.                                   │
  │  Other tools tell you what to see when you get there.           │
  │                                                                 │
  │  Used with that understanding, they are indispensable.          │
  │  Used as a replacement for qualitative understanding,           │
  │  they lead to confident, well-evidenced wrong conclusions.      │
  │                                                                 │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```
