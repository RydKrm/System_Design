## Recall: Retrieval Without External Cues

### What Recall Is

Recall is the process of retrieving information from long-term memory without the presence of the target information itself. In a recall task, you must generate the answer from internal memory resources alone. The cue that initiates the retrieval is internal — a question, a context, a need — but the target information is not present in the environment.

There are two subtypes of recall that differ in the richness of the retrieval cue provided. **Free recall** is the most demanding form: retrieve everything you can from memory about a given topic, with no structural help. "Name all the features of this application from memory." **Cued recall** provides a partial cue that constrains the retrieval search space: "The backup feature — what are the keyboard shortcuts for it?"

Both forms of recall are significantly more cognitively demanding than recognition because they require the brain to perform a **generative search** of long-term memory — actively constructing candidate answers and checking them against an internal criterion of correctness. This generative search process is effortful, error-prone, and sensitive to interference from competing memories, current cognitive load, stress, and time elapsed since encoding.

### The Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve

In 1885, German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus published the first systematic study of memory and forgetting. By memorizing lists of nonsense syllables and measuring his own recall at various time intervals, Ebbinghaus established the **forgetting curve** — a mathematical description of how rapidly and predictably newly learned information is lost from memory in the absence of review.

The forgetting curve is exponential in shape: the rate of forgetting is fastest immediately after learning and slows over time. Roughly 50% of new information is forgotten within the first hour. Another 20% is lost within the first day. By the end of a week, approximately 80% of a single exposure to new material is gone from recall memory. This is a brutal fact about human memory that every designer must hold in mind — because it means that any information a user encountered once, without significant elaboration or repetition, is probably not available for recall just days later.
