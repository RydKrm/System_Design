# Hick's Law — Cognitive Psychology for UX Design

> _Everything a designer must know about decision time, cognitive load, and the science of choice — explained from first principles to practical application._

---

## Introduction — The Moment Before Every Tap

Picture a person standing in front of a vending machine with 150 items. They are hungry and want a snack. They scan the machine. Dozens of options present themselves simultaneously. Their eyes move from row to row. Their brain begins processing: chips or cookies? Sweet or salty? Which brand? What price? They stand there for thirty, forty, sixty seconds. Their hunger is not complicated. Their snack preference is clear. Yet the act of _choosing_ has become an exhausting ordeal.

Now picture the same person walking into a small cafe that serves one thing: the house sandwich. They walk in, they sit down, a sandwich arrives. They are satisfied in minutes.

The second experience was not less because it offered less. It was _better_ because it demanded less from the person's brain. The cognitive work of deciding was removed, and what remained was the experience itself.

This is Hick's Law in the most visceral, human terms. And it is one of the most important principles in all of UX design — not because it is complicated, but because it is so fundamental that once you truly understand it, you cannot design an interface again without thinking about it.

## Part 1 — What Hick's Law Is

### The Formal Statement

Hick's Law — also called the Hick-Hyman Law — is a psychological principle that describes the relationship between the number of choices presented to a person and the time it takes them to make a decision. In its formal statement:

**The time required to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number of choices available.**

In mathematical form: **T = b × log₂(n + 1)**

Where T is the decision time, n is the number of choices, and b is an empirically derived constant that depends on the difficulty of each choice and the person's familiarity with the task.

The critical word here is _logarithmically_. This is not a linear relationship. Decision time does not simply increase by the same amount for each additional option. Instead, it follows a logarithmic curve — meaning that adding choices hurts most when you have few choices already, and adding more choices to an already long list hurts progressively less. Going from 2 choices to 4 choices approximately doubles decision time. Going from 20 choices to 22 choices adds very little. This mathematical reality has profound implications for how designers should think about reducing choices in their interfaces.

### The History — Two Scientists, One Insight

The law is named after two researchers who worked independently and arrived at overlapping conclusions. The first was William Edmund Hick, a British psychologist who published his foundational paper in 1952, titled "On the rate of gain of information." Hick conducted experiments in which participants responded to one of several possible stimuli (such as lights or sounds) by pressing a corresponding button. He measured the time between stimulus presentation and correct response across varying numbers of stimulus-response pairs. His finding: as the number of possible stimulus-response pairs doubled, reaction time increased by a consistent, predictable amount.

The second was Ray Hyman, an American psychologist who published complementary work in 1953, examining how uncertainty — not just number of choices, but also the probability distribution of choices — affected reaction time. Hyman's contribution deepened the understanding by showing that decision time was related not just to the count of options but to the _information content_ of the choice — how much uncertainty needed to be resolved.

Together, their work established what is now called the Hick-Hyman Law, though in UX design it is almost universally referred to simply as Hick's Law.

### The Information Theory Foundation

To truly understand why Hick's Law works, it is necessary to understand the information-theoretic framework that Hick and Hyman both drew upon. They were working in the years immediately following Claude Shannon's revolutionary information theory (1948), which provided a mathematical framework for quantifying _uncertainty_ and _information_.

In information theory, the amount of information required to identify one item among n equally probable items is log₂(n) bits. This is why the formula uses log₂ — the brain is essentially processing a binary decision tree, repeatedly halving the space of possible answers until it arrives at a choice. A binary choice (yes/no, left/right) requires resolving 1 bit of uncertainty. Four equally likely choices require 2 bits (because you need 2 binary decisions to arrive at one of 4 options). Sixteen equally likely choices require 4 bits.

The brain's decision process, according to this model, is serial and binary at its deepest level — it cannot evaluate all n options simultaneously in constant time. It must sequentially narrow the option space, and each narrowing step takes some fixed amount of time. More choices mean more narrowing steps, and therefore more time.

This information-theoretic grounding is what gives Hick's Law its mathematical precision and its surprising durability. It is not just an empirical observation from a few experiments. It is grounded in the fundamental mathematics of how information and uncertainty work — which is why it generalizes so well across wildly different contexts, from button-pressing experiments in a 1952 laboratory to smartphone UI in 2024.

## Part 2 — The Cognitive Psychology Beneath Hick's Law

### Working Memory — The Brain's Limited Workspace

To understand why too many choices create cognitive load, you must understand working memory — the mental system that temporarily holds and manipulates information during active thought. Working memory is the brain's equivalent of RAM. It is fast, flexible, and absolutely essential for complex thought — but it has a fundamental limitation that has been known since 1956, when psychologist George Miller published his famous paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two."

Miller's finding was that human working memory can hold approximately 7 ± 2 "chunks" of information at once. Later research has revised this estimate downward to approximately 4 chunks for complex cognitive tasks. When you present a user with a navigation menu containing 12 items, their working memory must hold all 12 items while scanning them, comparing them to their goal, and making a selection. But working memory can only hold 4. The system is immediately overloaded. Items are dropped and re-read. The scanning process becomes slower and more effortful. Cognitive load increases.

### The Paradox of Choice — When More Options Cause Paralysis

In 2000, psychologists Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper conducted a now-famous study that has become one of the most cited examples of Hick's Law in real-world consumer behavior. They set up a jam-tasting table at a gourmet grocery store, presenting either 6 jams or 24 jams on different days, and measured how many customers stopped to sample and, crucially, how many actually purchased.

The results were striking: the larger display with 24 jams attracted more attention — 60% of passing customers stopped to look. The smaller display with 6 jams attracted only 40%. But when it came to purchases, the smaller display dramatically outperformed: 30% of those who stopped at the 6-jam display made a purchase, while only 3% of those who stopped at the 24-jam display bought anything.

More options created more interest but dramatically less action. The cognitive burden of choosing among 24 options was so high that most people preferred to make no choice at all rather than risk making a suboptimal one. This phenomenon — **choice paralysis** or **analysis paralysis** — is one of the most important behavioral consequences of violating Hick's Law, and it is everywhere in digital product design.

### Cognitive Load Theory — The Three Types of Mental Effort

Cognitive load theory, developed by educational psychologist John Sweller in the 1980s, provides another important lens for understanding Hick's Law. Sweller identified three types of cognitive load that compete for limited mental resources.

**Intrinsic load** is the mental effort required by the task itself — the inherent complexity of what the user is trying to accomplish. Filling in a tax form has high intrinsic load because taxes are genuinely complex. Sending a message to a friend has low intrinsic load because the task is simple.

**Extraneous load** is the mental effort created by poorly designed interfaces — unnecessary complexity introduced by bad design choices rather than by the inherent difficulty of the task. This is the type of cognitive load that Hick's Law directly addresses. A 12-item navigation menu creates extraneous load for a task that could be accomplished with a 5-item menu. The extra 7 items do not make the task harder — they make the _interface_ harder. The user must expend mental energy on the interface rather than on their actual goal.

**Germane load** is the productive mental effort required to form new understandings and long-term memories — the "good" cognitive load of learning. When a user encounters a new design pattern, some germane load is expected and appropriate as they learn the interface.

The implication of cognitive load theory for Hick's Law is that good design minimizes extraneous load (the interface getting in the way) while respecting intrinsic load (the genuine complexity of the task) and allowing appropriate germane load (learning the interface once, so it becomes effortless).

### The Serial Position Effect — How We Scan Choice Lists

When humans scan a list of choices, they do not evaluate every item with equal attention. Decades of memory and attention research have established the **serial position effect** — the tendency to remember and attend to items at the beginning of a list (primacy effect) and items at the end of a list (recency effect), while items in the middle are least well remembered and least well attended.

This has direct design implications when choices must be presented in a list. The most important options should be at the top or bottom of a list, never buried in the middle. In navigation bars with 5 items, the first and last items receive more attention than the middle. In dropdown menus, the first few options and the last option are most often noticed. In settings menus, items in the middle of long lists are frequently overlooked.

## Part 3 — Hick's Law in Action: UX Design Applications

### Application 1 — Navigation Design

Navigation is the most direct application of Hick's Law in UI design. Every navigation menu, tab bar, sidebar, and drawer is essentially a list of choices, and the time it takes a user to find their destination is governed directly by Hick's Law.

The empirical research consistently shows that 5–7 navigation items is the optimal range for most applications. Below 3 items, the navigation may not cover the full scope of the application. Above 7 items, cognitive load increases significantly. The 5-item limit for mobile tab bars is not an arbitrary convention — it is grounded in both working memory research (Miller's Law: 7 ± 2) and Hick's Law (each additional item past 5 adds meaningfully to decision time while providing diminishing navigational value).

But Hick's Law in navigation goes beyond counting items. It is about the **quality of differentiation** between items. Five navigation items that all sound similar ("Learn," "Discover," "Explore," "Browse," "Find") create essentially the same cognitive load as ten items, because the brain must work harder to distinguish between them. Five items with clearly distinct, non-overlapping purposes ("Home," "Search," "Cart," "Orders," "Profile") are processed much faster because each item is unambiguously different from every other.

```

  BAD (5 items, ambiguous):

  │  [ Learn ] [ Explore ] [ Discover ] [ Browse ] [ Find ]  │
  │                                                          │
```

### Application 2 — Forms and Input Reduction

Forms are one of the most friction-intensive parts of any digital product. Every field in a form is a decision: what to enter, how to format it, whether it is required. Every additional field increases both the intrinsic load (filling in actual information) and the extraneous load (deciding how to fill the field, reading labels, understanding requirements). Hick's Law applies because even if each field is not a "choice" in the traditional sense, the cumulative cognitive demand of processing and responding to multiple simultaneous requirements follows the same logarithmic fatigue pattern.

The research on form abandonment confirms this: for every field added to a form above the minimum necessary, form completion rates drop measurably. Studies by Marketo, HubSpot, and others have consistently found that reducing form fields from 11 to 4 can increase completion rates by 120% or more. The principle of progressive disclosure — asking for the minimum information to complete the current step, then asking for more information later — is a direct application of Hick's Law to form design.

### Application 3 — Onboarding and Feature Reveal

One of the most common UX mistakes in product design — particularly in mobile apps — is showing all features to new users immediately. The thinking behind this is understandable: designers are proud of their features and want users to see everything the product can do. But under Hick's Law, this is catastrophically counterproductive.

A new user encountering an app for the first time has zero familiarity with its patterns, zero mental model of its structure, and a limited amount of patience. Presenting them with a full-featured interface, with every feature visible and every option accessible, maximizes their cognitive load at precisely the moment when their cognitive resources are most taxed by the unfamiliarity of everything.

The correct approach is to reveal features progressively — showing only the features relevant to the user's immediate goal, then gradually exposing additional capabilities as the user becomes more familiar with the core experience. This is not hiding features. It is _sequencing_ them to match the user's cognitive readiness.

### Application 4 — Visual Search and Interface Scanning

Hick's Law applies not only to explicit choices (menu items, buttons, options) but to the broader challenge of visual search — finding a specific element within a cluttered visual field. When an interface has many competing visual elements — multiple calls to action, many icons, dense text, inconsistent spacing — the user's brain must evaluate many more "candidates" before identifying their target. This increases the cognitive work of using the interface even when the user is not making a discrete choice.

This is why the principle of **visual hierarchy** (which we have studied previously) is so deeply connected to Hick's Law. Visual hierarchy reduces the effective number of choices the user must evaluate by creating clear levels of importance. If the primary CTA button is visually dominant and all other elements are visually subordinate, the user's brain does not need to evaluate every element equally — the hierarchy pre-selects the most likely candidate, effectively reducing n to 1 or 2 even in a complex interface.

### Application 5 — Smart Defaults and Pre-Selection

One of the most powerful and underused applications of Hick's Law is the strategic use of defaults. A **default** is a pre-selected option that applies if the user takes no action. Defaults reduce the decision burden because the user only needs to decide if they _disagree_ with the default — they do not need to make an active choice for the most common case.

This is the principle behind "recommended" plans being pre-selected on pricing pages, notifications being on by default, shipping addresses being remembered, and form fields being pre-populated. Each default removes one choice from the user's cognitive burden. For users whose preference matches the default (usually the majority), the effective n for that decision is 0.

However, defaults come with significant ethical responsibility. Using defaults to coerce users into choices that benefit the company rather than the user — pre-selecting more expensive plans, defaulting users into email marketing, defaulting to data-sharing — is a **dark pattern** that violates trust and user autonomy. The ethical use of defaults is to pre-select the option that is objectively best for most users, not the option that is best for the business.

## Part 4 — Limitations and Nuances of Hick's Law

### When More Choices Are the Right Design Decision

Hick's Law is a principle, not an absolute rule. There are important situations where offering more choices is correct and necessary, and applying Hick's Law dogmatically can lead to designs that are overly constrained or even counterproductive.

The most important limiting case is **expert users with clear goals**. Hick's Law is most powerful when users are uncertain about what they want or unfamiliar with the interface. Expert users who know exactly what they are looking for benefit from comprehensive option sets — they can use visual scanning and pattern matching to quickly locate their target in a dense list. A professional video editor using Premiere Pro does not want their hundreds of effects filtered down to five "easy" choices. A power user in a developer console does not want their settings simplified to five options. For these users, reducing choices would remove functionality they depend on.

The mitigating factor is **familiarity**. Hick's original research showed that the b constant in the formula decreases significantly with practice. A user who has seen a 12-item menu dozens of times processes it faster than a user seeing it for the first time, because they have built a spatial memory of the menu — they know roughly where their target is before they even begin reading. This is why the argument "our power users need all these options" is often valid for well-established products with a loyal user base, but dangerous for new products or for onboarding flows.

### The Chunking Strategy — Organizing Rather Than Eliminating

When choices cannot be reduced — when every option is genuinely necessary — Hick's Law can still be honored through **chunking**. Chunking is the cognitive process of grouping individual items into meaningful categories, which are then processed as single units rather than individual items. George Miller's research on working memory established that while working memory holds 4–7 individual items, it can hold 4–7_chunks_, where each chunk can contain many individual elements.

In design, chunking means organizing a long list of choices into a small number of clearly named categories. A navigation menu with 12 items becomes 3 groups of 4. A settings page with 30 settings becomes 5 sections of 6. A product catalog with 500 items becomes 8 categories of ~60. The user first chooses a category (a decision among 3–8 options, well within working memory capacity), then chooses within the category (another decision among ~4–8 options). Two quick decisions replace one overwhelming decision.

## Part 5 — Hick's Law and Human Psychology — The Deeper Connection

### Decision Fatigue — Why Choices Accumulate in Cost

Each decision a user makes in the course of using a product depletes a finite psychological resource. This phenomenon — **decision fatigue** — was documented extensively by researcher Roy Baumeister and his colleagues, and has been replicated in contexts ranging from judicial sentencing (judges make more favorable decisions in the morning, harsher ones in the afternoon as decision fatigue sets in) to consumer purchasing (shoppers who have made more decisions earlier in a shopping trip make worse purchasing decisions later).

In UX design, decision fatigue compounds the effects of Hick's Law across a user's entire session. Each choice the user makes — however small — consumes some of this finite resource. By the time the user reaches the checkout page at the end of a shopping flow, if they have been making dozens of micro-decisions throughout their session (which filter to apply, which product to tap, which photos to look at, whether to read the description), they have significantly less cognitive capacity for the final, most important decision (whether to complete the purchase).

This is why streamlining early decisions in a user flow — reducing unnecessary choices in navigation, in category browsing, in product comparison — does not just make those early interactions better. It **preserves cognitive resources for the most critical decision** at the end of the flow.

### The Emotional Dimension — Anxiety, Regret, and Trust

Hick's Law has a psychological dimension that goes beyond mere cognitive load: the **emotional cost of choosing**. Barry Schwartz, in his 2004 book "The Paradox of Choice," identified two emotional dynamics that are amplified by excessive choice and that directly affect user satisfaction and trust.

The first is **anticipatory anxiety** — the distress that precedes a decision when the chooser is uncertain about which option is best. With more choices, the probability of making a suboptimal choice increases in the user's mind, creating proportionally greater anxiety. A user choosing between 2 plans experiences mild uncertainty. A user choosing between 8 plans experiences significant anxiety. In a digital product context, this anxiety manifests as hesitation, excessive comparison, requesting reviews and recommendations, and ultimately, abandonment.

The second is **post-choice regret** — the disappointment after a decision when the chooser wonders whether they made the right choice. With more options available, the forgone alternatives (the things you could have chosen but didn't) loom larger. The more options exist, the more "roads not taken" there are, and the more opportunity there is to imagine that one of those untaken roads was better. This is the "what if" phenomenon that causes buyers' remorse, app uninstalls, and subscription cancellations.

By reducing choices, designers reduce both anticipatory anxiety (there's less to be uncertain about) and post-choice regret (there are fewer alternative paths to mourn). This is why Hick's-Law-compliant design does not just improve task completion metrics — it genuinely improves how users _feel_ about the product.

## Part 6 — Hick's Law in Practice: A Complete Design Framework

### The ABCDE Framework for Applying Hick's Law

Every design decision about choices can be evaluated through five lenses that together constitute a complete framework for applying Hick's Law in practice.

**A — Audit.** Count every choice the user must make in a given flow or screen. Literally count the interactive elements, the form fields, the navigation items, the options, the checkboxes, the toggles. Most designers dramatically underestimate the number of choices they are presenting. See the actual number, unfiltered.

**B — Baseline.** Determine which choices are genuinely necessary for the user to accomplish their goal. Not nice-to-have, not beneficial to the business, not potentially useful — strictly necessary. Every choice that does not meet this bar is a candidate for removal, hiding, or deferral.

**C — Chunk.** For choices that cannot be removed, organize them into meaningful categories. Apply the working memory principle — no more than 5–7 items in any displayed group. Use labels, sections, and visual separators to make the grouping perceptually clear.

**D — Default.** For choices where a sensible default exists, pre-select it. Ensure all defaults genuinely serve the user's most common need, not the product's commercial interest. Confirm that all defaults are easily reversible.

**E — Evaluate.** Test the design with real users. Measure decision time, error rate, completion rate, and self-reported confusion. The numbers will tell you whether your application of Hick's Law is working. Iterate based on evidence.

```
  the user must make is a choice they WANT to make,
  not one the interface is forcing upon them.

  Hick's Law is ultimately about respect:
  respect for the user's time, their cognitive
  resources, their attention, and their patience.
  Every unnecessary choice you remove is an act
  of respect. Every unnecessary choice you add
  is a small act of disrespect.
```

## Conclusion — What Hick's Law Really Teaches Us

Hick's Law is often summarized as "fewer choices are better." This summary is useful but incomplete. The deeper truth is more nuanced and more powerful: **every choice has a cost, and that cost should be justified by the value the choice delivers to the user**.

Some choices are genuinely valuable — the user benefits from being able to customize, from being able to control, from being able to choose. These choices should be provided and designed thoughtfully. Other choices are accidental — they exist not because the user needs them but because the designer did not think carefully about what the user actually needs. These choices should be eliminated. Still other choices are excessive — providing 12 options when 3 or 4 would serve all real user needs. These should be reduced.

The cognitive psychology underlying Hick's Law reveals a fundamental truth about human beings: we are not choice-maximizing machines. We are meaning-seeking, effort-minimizing creatures who are trying to accomplish goals — not to evaluate all possible options. When a design respects this reality, it does not feel limiting. It feels liberating. The well-designed interface that presents exactly the choices the user needs, at exactly the moment they need them, in exactly the right structure and order, creates an experience that feels effortless, intelligent, and trustworthy.

This is why the best digital products in the world — from the simplest consumer apps to the most sophisticated professional tools — are united by one quality: they do not ask you to do more thinking than the task requires. They handle the cognitive work so you don't have to. They reduce the decisions to the ones that matter. And they make those remaining decisions as easy and clear and confident as possible.

That is what Hick's Law is really about. Not simplicity for its own sake. Respect for the human mind.
